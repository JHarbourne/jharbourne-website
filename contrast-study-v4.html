Colour Contrast Perception

Can you tell us which text is easier to read?

Current web accessibility standards (WCAG) state that text must achieve a 4.5:1 contrast ratio – a single fixed number applied to every colour. But does your eye actually work that way?

This study investigates whether the colour (hue) of a background affects the perceived readability of text – independently of the standard luminance-based ratio. Your responses will help us understand whether the current standard captures real human perception across the full colour spectrum.

The study takes approximately 10–15 minutes and has two short blocks. You will be shown pairs of text samples and asked to click the one that looks easier to read – like an optician's chart, but for colour contrast.

What is collected – and what isn't

This study collects your responses, response times, and some brief questions about your vision and display conditions. It does not collect your name, email address, or IP address. Raw data files will be deleted once statistical analysis is complete; only anonymised aggregate findings will be published.

Block 2: Does a WCAG-failing grey feel as readable as a WCAG-passing coloured text?

Text A = same-hue chromatic at ~4.5:1 (WCAG AA pass)  ·  Text B = neutral grey at ~3.0:1 (WCAG fail). WCAG predicts A should always look clearer. If you sometimes preferred B, same-hue colour combinations may be harder to read than their WCAG score suggests.

What to look for: If you chose the grey (B) more than expected by chance, the same-hue chromatic text — despite passing WCAG at 4.5:1 — felt no clearer than a neutral grey at 3.0:1. This suggests that WCAG's formula overestimates the readability of same-hue colour-on-colour combinations, because it only measures luminance, not the chromatic relationship.
What this study is about: WCAG 2.x defines a minimum contrast ratio of 4.5:1, calculated purely from luminance – but emerging research suggests that hue also affects perceived readability. This experiment collects real perceptual data to test that hypothesis across the full colour spectrum.
